Introduction

A central challenge in quantum information science is how to scale quantum information processing beyond individual devices and to make effective use of the resulting large-scale systems. The Center advances theoretical research that addresses this challenge by studying quantum networks and distributed quantum computation as general, implementation-independent frameworks for scalable quantum information processing. We also investigate quantum algorithms and protocols that clarify how computational advantages can be systematically extracted when quantum computation is extended across multiple, interconnected quantum systems. These scaling-oriented studies are complemented by foundational research on quantum control and non-classicality, which underpin all quantum information processing.

Quantum Networks: Foundation for Scalable Quantum Information Processing

As quantum information processing systems grow in size and complexity, scaling beyond individual devices becomes a fundamental challenge. Quantum networks provide a general framework in which multiple quantum systems can be interconnected and jointly exploited as a larger computational and information-processing resource. We investigate the theoretical foundations of quantum networks as an enabling structure for scalable quantum information processing, including abstract models of quantum interconnects, the capabilities and limitations imposed by networked architectures, and the fundamental differences between isolated and network-embedded quantum systems.

Principles of Distributed Quantum Computation

This research explores how quantum computation can be performed across multiple, physically separated quantum nodes. We study universal principles governing distributed quantum operations, including how quantum tasks can be decomposed across nodes, how non-local correlations and communication enable joint operations, and what fundamental constraints arise when computation is carried out in a spatially distributed manner. Through these studies, we aim to clarify how the structure of distributed quantum computation differs from that of monolithic devices, and how such structures can be systematically exploited to extend the reach and functionality of quantum computation.

Operational Characterization of Quantum Resources

We study the operational roles of quantum resources—such as entanglement, quantum memory, and quantum and classical communication—in distributed and networked settings. Unlike standard entanglement-based frameworks that treat local operations and storage as freely available, our work explicitly accounts for the costs and limitations associated with memory, communication, and coordination across separated systems. The goal is to clarify which resources are required to implement quantum operations across distributed systems, how different resources can be transformed or combined, and how their availability fundamentally constrains achievable tasks.

Network-Aware Quantum Algorithms and Protocols

We study quantum algorithms and protocols that are explicitly designed for distributed and networked settings, where computation and communication are intertwined. Rather than assuming idealized, monolithic processors, this work investigates how quantum tasks can be structured across multiple nodes, how non-local operations can be coordinated, and how algorithmic performance is affected by the availability and distribution of quantum resources. The goal is to identify general principles that govern the effective use of large-scale, networked quantum systems.

System-Level Evaluation of Quantum Network Components

This research develops theoretical frameworks for assessing the performance of quantum nodes when they operate as components of a larger quantum network. We study figures of merit, error models, and performance trade-offs that arise specifically in distributed settings, providing principled tools for comparing architectures and understanding how local device characteristics impact network-level functionality.

High-Fidelity Modeling and Simulation of Quantum Systems

We develop theory-driven modeling and simulation frameworks that aim to capture the microscopic dynamics of quantum devices interacting with quantum fields in network-relevant settings. A central challenge is achieving sufficient accuracy to faithfully represent error mechanisms, correlations, and dynamical effects that directly impact large-scale and fault-tolerant quantum information processing. To this end, we study efficient methods for simulating high-dimensional quantum systems, balancing computational tractability with the level of precision required for reliable device modeling and system-level analysis.

Quantum Control and Manipulation

Controlling quantum systems with precision is key to unlocking their full potential. Our research explores fundamental quantum control tasks to uncover the principles that govern quantum information processing. However, real-world implementations are inevitably affected by control errors and environmental decoherence. We investigate how these imperfections impact our ability to manipulate quantum systems and how to mitigate them. Through these efforts, we aim to develop more effective control strategies, paving the way for more powerful and robust quantum information processing.

Non-Classicality in Quantum Systems.

Quantum mechanics reveals phenomena that challenge classical intuition, showcasing the unique nature of quantum systems. We explore the fundamental principles of quantum non-classicality and how they manifest in the manipulation of quantum states. By deepening our understanding of these principles, we aim to uncover new ways to harness quantum mechanics for information processing that transcends classical limitations.
